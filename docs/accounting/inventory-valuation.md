# Inventory Valuation

## Quick Summary

Inventory valuation determines the dollar value of inventory on the balance sheet. The valuation method — average cost, standard cost, FIFO — affects both the balance sheet and the income statement through COGS.

## Difficulty

Intermediate

## Estimated Time

15 minutes

## Overview

Inventory is an asset on the balance sheet. When inventory is sold, its value moves from the balance sheet (asset) to the income statement (COGS). The valuation method determines how that cost flows through.

## Valuation and COGS

```
Beginning Inventory
  + Purchases
  - Ending Inventory (physical count or system)
  = Cost of Goods Sold

Beg Inventory: $100,000
  + Purchases:   $50,000
  - End Inventory: $80,000
  = COGS:       $70,000
```

## The Impact of Valuation

| Situation | Inventory Asset | COGS | Gross Profit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rising costs, FIFO | Higher | Lower | Higher |
| Rising costs, Average | Middle | Middle | Middle |
| Rising costs, LIFO | Lower | Higher | Lower |

## Common Customer Questions

**Q**: The inventory value on the balance sheet dropped, but quantities stayed the same. Why? **Common Causes**: Cost decreased due to a lower-cost receipt, inventory adjustments with a value change, standard cost revaluation.

**Q**: COGS seems too high this month. What records to inspect? **Records to Inspect**: Item fulfillment quantities, average cost changes, large adjustments, work order costs.
