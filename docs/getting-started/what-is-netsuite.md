# What is NetSuite?

## Quick Summary

NetSuite is a cloud-based business management platform that combines accounting, ERP, CRM, ecommerce, and inventory management into a single system. It is used by over 40,000 organizations worldwide to run their core business processes.

## What is NetSuite?

NetSuite is an enterprise resource planning (ERP) system delivered as a cloud service. Instead of installing software on servers, you access NetSuite through a web browser. Oracle acquired NetSuite in 2016 and continues to develop and maintain the platform.

NetSuite is not just accounting software, though accounting is a central component. It is a broad business platform that can manage:

- Financial records and reporting
- Customer relationships (CRM)
- Sales orders and invoicing
- Purchasing and vendor management
- Inventory and warehousing
- Manufacturing and assembly
- Employee management (Payroll is typically handled by integrated partners)
- Ecommerce websites
- Reporting and business intelligence

## Why It Matters

Before ERP systems, businesses often used separate tools for accounting, inventory, sales, and customer management. A sales order might be entered in one system, inventory deducted in another, and the invoice created in a third. This led to duplicate data entry, errors, and delayed reporting.

NetSuite connects these processes into one shared system. A single sales order can:

- Check inventory availability
- Reserve or commit stock
- Trigger fulfillment
- Generate an invoice
- Update the customer record
- Post to the general ledger

This shared data model means reports reflect a single source of truth, and teams across the company work from the same information.

## Core Capabilities

### Financial Management

General ledger, accounts payable, accounts receivable, billing, fixed assets, revenue recognition, and multi-book accounting. Financial reports, budgets, and consolidations run on the same data as operational transactions.

### Customer Relationship Management (CRM)

Lead and prospect tracking, customer records, sales forecasting, campaign management, and customer support case management.

### Inventory Management

Item records, stock tracking across locations, transfer orders, adjustments, bin management, cycle counts, and replenishment. Supports multi-location and advanced inventory features.

### Order Management

Sales orders, quotes, returns, and fulfillment processing. Orders can flow from ecommerce, sales reps, or customer portals.

### Procurement

Purchase orders, vendor records, receiving, vendor returns, and approval workflows.

### Manufacturing

Assembly items, build orders, demand planning, and work orders. Supports basic and advanced manufacturing.

### Ecommerce

SuiteCommerce provides website, checkout, and customer portal capabilities integrated with back-end operations.

### Reporting and Analytics

Saved searches, reports, SuiteAnalytics workbooks, and dashboards. All reports draw from the same live data.

### Customization and Development

SuiteScript (JavaScript) for scripting, SuiteTalk for REST/SOAP API integrations, workflows for approvals, and custom fields, forms, and records.

## Who Uses NetSuite?

| Role | What They Do in NetSuite |
|---|---|
| Administrators | Configure features, manage users and roles, customize forms and fields |
| Accountants | Run financial reports, process invoices, manage the general ledger |
| Warehouse Staff | Receive inventory, transfer stock, fulfill orders |
| Sales Reps | Create quotes and orders, track customer activity |
| Purchasing Teams | Create purchase orders, receive shipments, manage vendors |
| Developers | Write SuiteScript, build integrations, create custom records |
| Executives | Review dashboards, KPIs, and financial reports |

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming NetSuite is just accounting**: NetSuite manages far more than finances. Limiting your view to accounting means missing operational benefits.
- **Thinking every user sees the same interface**: The menu, dashboard, records, and reports a user sees depend entirely on their role and permissions.
- **Skipping foundational training**: Jumping straight to SuiteScript or advanced reporting without understanding records, transactions, and roles leads to confusion.
- **Treating NetSuite as a set of disconnected modules**: Everything in NetSuite connects. An inventory change affects accounting. A sales order affects fulfillment. Understanding these connections is key.

## Best Practices

- Start with the Getting Started path before diving into specific modules.
- Learn the record types and transaction types used in your role.
- Use global search as your primary navigation tool.
- Customize your dashboard to show the information most relevant to your daily work.
- Understand how your role's permissions affect what you can see and do.
